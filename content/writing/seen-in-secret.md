*A longer companion to the reflection shared with my Wednesday fasting group, for the feast of Saint Charbel Makhlouf.*
{.preface}

I took Charbel for my confirmation name. Part of the reason is a saint. The rest of the reason is a grandmother. I will get to her.

This past December, Pope Leo XIV stood inside a small cave carved into the side of a mountain in Lebanon and asked: what is the legacy of a man who wrote nothing, who lived a hidden and silent life, yet whose fame spread throughout the world?[^1]

He was standing at the tomb of Saint Charbel Makhlouf, the first pope ever to do so.

The man baptized Youssef in 1828 was beatified by Pope Saint Paul VI in the closing days of the Second Vatican Council. While the Church debated its future, Paul VI held up a silent hermit. The Church, he told them, also needs men who give themselves to unceasing prayer, who teach us the value of poverty, penance, and asceticism, so that our souls might be liberated in their ascent to God.

Twelve years later, the same pope canonized him. Recalling how it was on Christmas Eve of 1898 that Charbel gave his soul to God, Paul VI observed that modern man can scarcely comprehend the severe austerity of such a hermetic life. Yet it was precisely through that life of mortification, obedience, chastity, and solitude that Charbel left great room for the Holy Spirit and received the wonders promised in the beatitudes: sweetness, humility, mercy, and the joy found only in the healing power of Christ.[^2]

For twenty-three years as a hermit, Charbel slept five hours a night on a mat with a wooden log for a pillow. He ate one meal a day. His life centered on the Mass he celebrated at noon. The rest of the day he spent in silent prayer, either preparing himself to receive the Eucharist or giving thanks for having received it.

## the light

Immediately after Charbel was laid to rest, locals began reporting a brilliant light emanating from the place of his tomb. It appeared first to the poor: the women and children, often illiterate and overlooked. It appeared finally to the Muslim administrator of the region and his soldiers, whom the star led through the night to the monastery in a light so bright they had no need of their torches. Night after night, Christians, Muslims, and Druze saw the light and began making pilgrimage to the tomb. The monks of the monastery were the most doubting; only after they exhumed the body of Brother Charbel and found it incorrupt did they begin to believe.

> God chose what is foolish in the world to shame the wise, God chose what is weak in the world to shame the strong, God chose what is low and despised in the world, even things that are not, to bring to nothing things that are. <cite>— 1 Corinthians 1:27–28</cite>

It is said that no one saw Saint Charbel's face fully during his lifetime, for he kept his eyes fixed on the ground except before the Eucharist and the Tabernacle. When he was found after entrusting his soul to God, his eyes were fixed upward, toward heaven, toward Christ. The light of God, seen through Saint Charbel's life and in his death, directs us toward God.

Just as God's light marked the path of the Muslim soldiers through the dark of night to a Maronite monastery in the mountains, Saint Charbel reminds us of the promise of the Lord:

> In the tender compassion of our God\
> the dawn from on high shall break upon us,\
> to shine on those who dwell in darkness and the shadow of death,\
> and to guide our feet into the way of peace. <cite>— Luke 1:78–79</cite>

Today, July 22, thousands of people from across the globe will process up into those mountains in faithful pilgrimage, in honor of and thanksgiving for the countless healings God has given through the intercession of Saint Charbel.[^3] The Maronite Church long kept his feast on July 23, the anniversary not of his death, as with most saints, but of his priestly ordination: the day he handed his life over. It is now celebrated on the third Sunday of July, and in the Latin Church on July 24.

## the quiet

Now, my grandmother. Helen Rose Latuff was the daughter of Lebanese Maronite immigrants. She married an Irish Catholic in the Latin Church, and it was through the quiet lens of her life that I first understood Catholic service and living. Without fanfare.

In the summers I would join her and the other dedicated women who diligently made the food and hosted the funeral receptions. I watched the small tokens: flowers placed in the church, the tidying between the big bi-annual church cleanings. The prayer circles. The dinners put together and dropped off when someone was ill. The monthly meals prepared and served at the Dorothy Day Center in St. Paul. It was through her that I most clearly saw the light of Christ.

## what is the legacy?

Pope Leo answered his own question: the Holy Spirit formed Charbel so that he could teach those who live without God how to pray, those who live immersed in noise how to be silent, those who live ostentatiously how to be modest, and those who seek riches how to be poor.

Not many of us are called to such a role in the mystical Body of Christ. Most of us are called to be the Grandma Helens of the world: the quiet, but no less radiating, lights of God.

Our little group fasts on Wednesdays. Nobody sees it. That's the design. But Charbel's cell was dark for twenty-three years, and God has made it a beacon to the world. Not many outside my small family remember Helen and her example. And that's okay too.

Whatever you're offering quietly, keep offering it.

It's in the chorus of lights that we are guided into the way of peace.
